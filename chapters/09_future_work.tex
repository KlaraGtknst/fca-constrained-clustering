\section{Conclusion and Future Work}
\label{sec:future_work}

The analysis of the consistency- and coherence-attices highlights the distinction between strict and relaxed notions of agreement between knowledge bases. 
The consistency-lattice retains only concepts whose extents coincide exactly in both the \ac{mlb}-constrained and the topic model-based lattices. 
In our case, no non-trivial document clusters are shared exactly between the two lattices, indicating that strict consistency is often too restrictive when comparing knowledge bases derived from different sources.

In contrast, the coherence-lattice allows partial overlap between concept extents by introducing a similarity threshold. 
The resulting lattice therefore captures document clusters that are largely shared between the two lattices, even when they do not coincide perfectly. 
The number of documents contained in coherence-lattice concepts provides an indication of the degree of agreement between the knowledge bases.
The \ac{mlb}-constrained lattice is derived from manually annotated labels and thus represents a high-precision conceptualization of the domain, albeit with limited hierarchical structure. 
By contrast, the knowledge base underlying the topic model-based lattice is data-driven and reflects statistical patterns in the document corpus, which may also include artefacts caused by noise or biases in the data.

Overall, the results suggest that practical knowledge integration requires tolerating a certain degree of inconsistency while maintaining sufficient coherence. The appropriate similarity threshold depends on the application domain: safety-critical areas such as medicine may require higher thresholds to ensure reliable knowledge alignment, whereas exploratory analysis may benefit from lower thresholds that allow broader, though less precise, overlaps.

Future work will investigate extending the approach to integrate more than two knowledge bases, determine how similarity thresholds should be adjusted to obtain coherence-lattices of comparable size, and assess whether Jaccard similarity is an appropriate metric for measuring overlap between concept extents across multiple lattices.
