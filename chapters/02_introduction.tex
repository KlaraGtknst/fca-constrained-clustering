\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

Concept hierarchies, i.e., systems of hyponym relationships~\cite{le2019inferring} with a node at the top level, form the backbone of expert ontologies such as WORDNET~\cite{miller1990wordnet,fellbaum1998wordnet}. % WORDNET: manual creation cf. le2019inferring, hearst1992hyponyms; hyponym = is-a relationship cf. hearst1992hyponyms
Concept hierarchies structure information into categories and enable the formulation of rules and relations, thereby facilitating the retrieval and reuse of knowledge bases~\cite{cimiano_learning_2005,yang2012personalized}. 
Manual creation of niche high-quality ontologies is expensive and limited to certain snapshots of evolving knowledge~\cite{li2018unsupervised}. 
In contrast, automatic concept hierarchy generation is not only more scalable but also enables concept hierarchies in areas not normally covered by existing expert ontologies.
While manual curation of expert ontologies can be considered a gold standard for concept hierarchies, it is often desirable to generate concept hierarchies that are sufficiently consistent with the hierarchical background knowledge of expert ontologies.

Automatic concept hierarchies generation usually relies on identifying explicit patterns in the text~\cite{le2019inferring}, for instance Hearst patterns~\cite{hearst1992hyponyms}, or \ac{nlp} tools extracting latent information from text followed by its representation in form of binary object-attribute matrices and \ac{fca}~\cite{cimiano_learning_2005}. 
Besides presentation purposes, \ac{fca} has been employed to identify potentially missing thesaurus entries in a specific expert ontology~\cite{zheng2020lexical,hao2021leveraging}. 

In order to guide automatic concept hierarchy generation, both human involvement~\cite{yang2012personalized} and \acp{llm}~\cite{kommineni2024from} have been utilized. 
This guidance has been implemented by restricting the generation of concept lattices with constraints~\cite{belohlavek2006formal,belohlavek2009formal}, or by incoorperating attribute weights~\cite{belohlavek2011selecting}.
Bade and Nürnberger~\cite{bade_personalized_2006,bade_hierarchical_2014} introduced the idea of using hierarchical constraints for generating concept hierarchies in a similarity space
created from text. 
Opposed to tarditional similarity-based concept hierarchy generation, \ac{fca}-based concept hierarchy generation can be considered a conceptual clustering technique which provides intensional descriptions for abstract concepts~\cite{cimiano_learning_2005}.

In this work, we propose integrating hierarchical background knowledge in \ac{fca} via \ac{mlb} constraints, which are a specific type of hierarchical constraint.
