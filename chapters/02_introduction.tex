\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

% WORDNET: manual creation cf. le2019inferring, hearst1992hyponyms; hyponym = is-a relationship cf. hearst1992hyponyms
Concept hierarchies form the backbone of expert ontologies such as WORDNET~\cite{miller1990wordnet,fellbaum1998wordnet}.
They are systems of hyponym relationships~\cite{le2019inferring} with a node at the top level.
Generally, they structure information into categories and enable the formulation of rules and relations, thereby facilitating the retrieval and reuse of knowledge bases~\cite{cimiano_learning_2005,yang2012personalized}. 
Manual creation of niche high-quality ontologies is expensive and limited to certain snapshots of evolving knowledge~\cite{li2018unsupervised}. 

In contrast, automatic concept hierarchy generation is not only more scalable but also enables concept hierarchies in areas not normally covered by existing expert ontologies.
Automatic concept hierarchy generation usually relies on identifying explicit patterns in the text~\cite{le2019inferring}, for instance Hearst patterns~\cite{hearst1992hyponyms}, or \ac{nlp} tools extracting latent information from text followed by its representation in the form of binary object-attribute matrices and \ac{fca}~\cite{cimiano_learning_2005}. 
Opposed to traditional similarity-based concept hierarchy generation, \ac{fca}-based concept hierarchy generation can be considered a conceptual clustering technique which provides intensional descriptions for abstract concepts~\cite{cimiano_learning_2005}.

While manual curation of expert ontologies can be considered a gold standard for concept hierarchies, it is often desirable to generate concept hierarchies to reduce cost and time.
However, such automatically generated hierarchies can conflict with existing expert ontologies leading to lack of trust in one or both ontologies.
In order to enforce generated hierarchies that are sufficiently consistent with the hierarchical background knowledge of expert ontologies, there is a need to guide and constrain generation.

Prior work on guidance mechanisms proposed human involvement~\cite{yang2012personalized} and \acp{llm}~\cite{kommineni2024from}. 
It has been implemented by restricting the generation of concept lattices with constraints~\cite{belohlavek2006formal,belohlavek2009formal}, or by incorporating attribute weights~\cite{belohlavek2011selecting}.
Bade and Nürnberger~\cite{bade_personalized_2006,bade_hierarchical_2014} introduced the idea of using hierarchical constraints for generating concept hierarchies in a similarity space
created from text. 

% Besides presentation purposes, \ac{fca} has been employed to identify potentially missing thesaurus entries in a specific expert ontology~\cite{zheng2020lexical,hao2021leveraging}. 

Up to now, systematic integration of hierarchical constraints via \ac{mlb} constraints into \ac{fca}-based hierarchy generation remains underexplored.
We propose to fill this gap by introducing \ac{mlb} constraints into the \ac{fca} framework for concept hierarchy generation.
Moreover, we present a case study on comparing \ac{fca}-based concept hierarchies generated with \ac{mlb} constraints to a second knowledge base based on corpus-specific statistical topic modeling.

% structure of the paper
The remainder of this paper is structured as follows: In Section~\ref{sec:related_work}, we review related work on concept hierarchy generation and the use of constraints in \ac{fca}. 
In Section~\ref{sec:foundations}, we provide the necessary background on \ac{fca} and \ac{mlb} constraints. 
In Section~\ref{sec:method}, we describe our proposed method for integrating hierarchical background knowledge in \ac{fca} via \ac{mlb} constraints. 
In Section~\ref{sec:case_study}, we present a case study comparing \ac{fca}-based concept hierarchies generated with \ac{mlb} constraints to a second knowledge base based on corpus-specific statistical topic modeling. 
Finally, in Section~\ref{sec:future_work}, we discuss potential future work and conclude the paper.
