\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

Concept hierarchies, i.e., systems of \textit{is-a} relationships~\cite{le2019inferring} with a node at the top level, form the backbone of expert ontologies such as WORDNET~\cite{miller1990wordnet,fellbaum1998wordnet}. % WORDNET: manual creation cf. le2019inferring, hearst1992hyponyms
Concept hierarchies structure information into categories and enable the formulation of rules and relations, thereby facilitating the retrieval and reuse of knowledge bases~\cite{cimiano_learning_2005}. 
Manual creation of niche high-quality ontologies is expensive and limited to certain snapshots of evolving knowledge~\cite{li2018unsupervised}. 
In contrast, automatic concept hierarchy generation is not only more scalable but also enables concept hierarchies in areas not normally covered by existing expert ontologies.

Concept hierarchies are usually generated using explicit patterns in the text - known as Hearst patterns~\cite{hearst1992hyponyms} - to extract concept hierarchies (e.g. ~\cite{le2019inferring}).
Alternatively, it is also possible to generate a concept hierarchy by using latent information from text and based on this, a concept hierarchy is calculated that does not
require the explicit occurrence of certain patterns in the text. It is possible to represent latent information from texts as a binary object attribute matrix~\cite{cimiano_learning_2005}
and to derive a concept hierarchy from this using Formal Concept Analysis (FCA)~\cite{fca-book}. The basic assumption behind the generation of latent concept hierarchies
is that individually generated artifacts consist of knowledge structures that are not present in expert ontologies. For this reason, generated concept hierarchies exclusively
cover knowledge structures that are not contained in expert ontologies. This view assumes that there is no overlap between the knowledge structure of expert ontologies and
generated concept hierarchies, which seems somewhat naive. A more realistic perspective is that expert ontologies have a certain degree of valid hierarchical background
knowledge and that generated concept hierarchies should be consistent with this.

The interplay between the generation of concept hierarchies based on FCA and expert ontologies has already been discussed. FCA has already been used to identify potentially
missing concepts in a specific expert ontology~\cite{zheng2020lexical,hao2021leveraging}. Since expert ontologies claim to be able to represent knowledge structures in general, domain-specific
use cases often cannot be adequately represented with them. An alternative strategy is therefore to generate concept hierarchies based on specific constraints. An early attempt
in this direction was the personalized generation of concept hierarchies, which enable manual guidance of the design process by the user~\cite{yang2012personalized} or more recently by incorporating
an LLM~\cite{kommineni2024from}. Bade and Nürnberger~\cite{bade_personalized_2006,bade_hierarchical_2014} introduced the idea of using hierarchical constraints for generating concept hierarchies in a similarity space
created from text. It have already shown that constraints can be used to restrict the generation of concept lattices~\cite{belohlavek2006formal,belohlavek2009formal}.
With the incorporation of attribute weights, manual curation of restrictions has already become superfluous~\cite{belohlavek2011selecting}.
Nevertheless, there is currently no approach that enables the integration of hierarchical constraints in FCA. In contrast to similarity-based concept hierarchy generation,
FCA-based concept hierarchy generation would explain the internal structure of a generated concept hierarchy that appeared in the text.
