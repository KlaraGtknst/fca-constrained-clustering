\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related_work}

\subsubsection{Data-Driven Approaches}
% 1. Data-driven concept hierarchy generation from text corpora
%% 1.1. Non-FCA data-driven approaches
Automatic concept hierarchy generation from text corpora has been approached in various ways.
Hierarchical text classification assigns documents one or more labels organized in a taxonomy~\cite{yu2022constrained}, which implicitly creates a hierarchical structure of documents based on their assigned labels. 
Approaches range from regularization-based classifiers~\cite{gopal2013recursive}, similarity-based matching of documents and labels in a common embedding space~\cite{li2019hiercon} to neural sequence models~\cite{risch2020hierarchical,yu2022constrained} and recent \acl{llm}-assisted taxonomy enrichment~\cite{zhang2025teleclass}.

%% 1.2. FCA-based data-driven approaches
Beyond classification-based approaches, research has explored deriving concept hierarchies directly from textual data using \ac{fca}. 
Since \ac{fca}-based concept hierarchy generation can be viewed as a form of conceptual clustering that yields intensional descriptions of abstract concepts through their intents~\cite{cimiano_learning_2005}, it provides a natural framework for constructing \textcolor{orange}{interpretable} hierarchies from document collections.
%% Text corpus as formal context: We use gutekunst2025conceptual, but with LDA instead of top2vec
In these approaches, a text corpus is represented as a formal context in which documents form the objects and features extracted from textual data serve as attributes.
Different methods have been proposed to derive such features, including bag-of-words representations capturing the presence of terms~\cite{cigarran_twitter_fca_2016}, \ac{lda}-based topic features~\cite{wang_topic_feature_2021,wang_topic_hierarchy_2023} or other topic model-based representations~\cite{Aznag2014LeveragingFC,gutekunst2025conceptual,hirth_ordinal_2024}. % hirth_ordinal_2024 uses own trained topic model refered to as SSH21, but is something trained using non-negative matrix factorization, gutekunst2025conceptual use top2vec, Aznag2014LeveragingFC: compared LDA, CTM, PLSA (found LDA to be best)

\subsubsection{Knowledge-Driven Approaches}
% 2. Knowledge-based concept hierarchy generation
While the above approaches derive hierarchical structures directly from data, other work incorporates prior knowledge or expert constraints to guide the hierarchy construction process.
% 2.1. Constrained Hierarchical Clustering -> explain MLB constraints
%% clustering with constraints
In constrained clustering, prior knowledge about the dataset is typically expressed through constraints. 
The simplest constraints are pairwise \textit{must-link} and \textit{cannot-link} constraints.
% , which require objects to be in the same or different clusters, respectively.
Conventional constrained clustering methods usually operate on a single level of granularity~\cite{wagstaff2001constrained}.
%% limits of pairwise constraints: hierarchical clustering
However, pairwise constraints become ambiguous in hierarchical clustering settings, where objects may appear in the same cluster at one level of the hierarchy but be separated at another~\cite{bade_hierarchical_2014}.
To address this limitation, Bade et al.~\cite{bade2006personalized,bade_creating_2008}  introduce \ac{mlb} constraints for \ac{hac}.
These \ac{mlb} constraints encode the order in which objects should be merged during agglomeration, effectively capturing relative hierarchical relationships~\cite{bade2006personalized,bade_creating_2008,bade_hierarchical_2014}.
\ac{mlb} constraints have been incorporated into hierarchical clustering in several ways, including modifying similarity measures during merging~\cite{bade2006personalized}, delaying merges until constraint violations are resolved~\cite{zhao2010hierarchical}, or integrating them into matrix-based approaches~\cite{zheng2011semi}.

% 2.2. Constrained and Knowledge-Guided FCA
Several works also investigate incorporating constraints or expert knowledge directly into \ac{fca}.
% constrained FCA != we use MLB constraints
Constraints have been used, for example, to encode mutually exclusive attributes~\cite{hidayat_constrained_fca_2024}
% we use support, i.e., the number of objects sharing a set of attributes, while belohlavek2009formal define importance directly on attributes, or belohlavek2006formal constrain closure operators, or mao_constrained_fca_2017 define a operator S (!= closure operator) to determine interesting attribute sets
or to prune the concept lattice to retain only concepts whose intents are considered important. %~\cite{belohlavek2006formal,belohlavek2009formal,mao_constrained_fca_2017}.
The latter approaches differ in how importance is defined, for example through attribute properties~\cite{belohlavek2009formal,hidayat_constrained_fca_2024}, constraints on the closure operator~\cite{belohlavek2006formal}, or additional operators~\cite{mao_constrained_fca_2017}.
% Attribute Exploration with contradicting experts: != we do not only have shared implications but also some-what shared (i.e., coherence lattice)
Beyond constraint-based pruning, research has also explored extending Attribute Exploration to settings with multiple, potentially contradicting or incomplete experts.
These extensions aim to derive implications that are shared across different subsets of experts~\cite{felde2021triadic,felde2022attribute}.

%% Our work
However, prior work has incorporated hierarchical \ac{mlb} constraints primarily into traditional clustering approaches and not into \ac{fca}-based hierarchy construction.
To the best of our knowledge, no prior work systematically compares concept lattices derived from data-driven probabilistic topic models with those obtained from constraint-based hierarchical knowledge bases.
